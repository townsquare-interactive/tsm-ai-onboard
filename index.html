Watch out
Three traps that
catch people off guard.

Most AI policy violations happen by accident, through tools people don't realize are capturing data. Tap each one to see the full detail.

1
Meeting notetakers
+
Don't let them join your calls. Apps that auto-join meetings and transcribe them are Conditional or Prohibited tools. When they record a call, that transcript almost certainly contains confidential or non-public information — which means you've put restricted data into an unapproved tool, even if you didn't type a single thing.

2
AI browser extensions
+
Route to IT — don't self-install. AI-powered browser extensions often read everything on your screen: emails, documents, internal dashboards, all of it. They're Prohibited until formally reviewed. If you find one you want, send a tool-review request to the AI Governance Committee — don't install it yourself.
3
AI inside tools you already use
+
Check before you enable. Word, Excel, Slack, Salesforce, your email client — all of them are adding AI features. These may not be covered under your existing license agreement or may have different data-handling terms than your approved company tools. Check with IT before switching any of them on.

The whiteboard.

Everything in your current conversation — your messages, uploaded files, every exchange — is visible to Claude all at once. This is the context window. It's large (think: a short novel), but it only lasts for this session.

When you start a new chat, it wipes completely. Claude has no recollection of your previous conversations unless Memory or Projects are in play.

Like a conference room whiteboard — everything's there while the meeting is running. Walk out, someone wipes it. Next person starts fresh.

The sticky note.

Memory is what persists across conversations. Your role, your preferences, how you like work formatted — things you'd otherwise have to re-explain every session. You build it by telling Claude what to carry forward.

Not everything belongs in memory. Stable, recurring facts do. Task-specific details live in the conversation itself, not in memory.

If the context window is a whiteboard, memory is a sticky note on the monitor — already there before you type a single word.

Six prompting principles — apply these immediately
Lead with context
Give Claude your role, the audience, and the goal before asking for anything. The more it knows about the situation, the more precisely it can help.
→ "I'm a Sales Manager writing to a client who missed two payments. Tone: firm but relationship-preserving. Under 120 words."
Assign a role
"Act as a sharp copy editor" or "You're a meticulous financial analyst" frames Claude's response mode before it starts. Role prompts measurably improve output quality.
→ "You are an experienced HR professional. Review this policy for tone and clarity."
Show an example
Paste an example of the format or style you want. "Write something in this style: [example]" is often more effective than trying to describe the format in words.
→ "Here's an example of how I like this formatted: [paste]. Now write one for [new topic]."
Iterate in-session
Don't start a new chat when output is wrong. Correct it in the same conversation. Claude uses your feedback to refine — starting over means starting from scratch.
→ "Good structure, but the opening paragraph is too formal. Rewrite just that section."
Break it into steps
Complex tasks done in one prompt produce generic results. Ask for an outline first, approve it, then ask for the full piece. Staged prompts give you control at every step.
→ First: "Give me 3 possible structures for this." Then: "Use structure 2. Now write section 1."
Ask it to self-check
Before relying on anything factual, ask Claude to flag what it's uncertain about. It will tell you. This surfaces errors before they reach other people.
→ "Review what you just wrote. Flag any claims you're not fully confident about."
Common mistakes — and what to do instead
1
Starting a new chat every session
+
Every time you open a fresh chat, Claude's whiteboard clears. You lose all the context you established — your role, the project background, previous decisions. People do this instinctively because it feels like a "clean start," but it means re-explaining the same setup every single time.
Instead: Keep working in the same conversation for ongoing work. Or create a Project so your context loads automatically.
2
Prompting like a search engine
+
Typing "how to handle a difficult client conversation" into Claude is like Googling it — you get generic advice. Claude can do significantly more when you give it the actual specifics: who the client is, what the situation is, what outcome you need.
Instead: Give it the real situation. "Client X is upset about delayed deliverables. I need to address it in a call tomorrow. They're relationship-sensitive. Help me prepare."
3
Not pushing back when the output is wrong
+
If Claude gets something wrong or produces something off-target, many people either accept it anyway or start over. Neither is right. Claude responds very well to specific correction — it uses your feedback to recalibrate, and the next output is almost always better.
Instead: Say what's specifically wrong. "The tone is too formal" or "This assumes the client already knows X — they don't. Rewrite with that in mind."
4
Sending AI output without reading it
+
AI-generated content can be fluent, confident, well-structured — and wrong. Hallucinated statistics, incorrect names, plausible-sounding facts that aren't true. The fluency of the output has no correlation with its accuracy.
Instead: Treat every AI output as a first draft that requires your review. Read it. Verify anything factual. Your name is on it once you send it.
5
Expecting Claude to know things it wasn't told
+
Claude doesn't have access to your emails, your calendar, your CRM, or anything else outside this conversation (unless those are explicitly connected tools). It only knows what you've told it in this session, or what's in your Memory or Project instructions.
Instead: Paste in the relevant information. "Here's the email thread. Here's the contract clause. Based on these, help me draft a response."
Quick reference — where AI helps most, and where to be careful
Claude excels at
  • Drafting and editing written communication
  • Summarizing long documents
  • Brainstorming options and frameworks
  • Reframing or restructuring existing content
  • Explaining complex topics in plain language
  • Writing and reviewing code
  • Synthesizing research across multiple sources
  • Preparing for meetings and presentations
Always verify when asking about
  • Specific statistics and numbers
  • Recent events (knowledge has a cutoff date)
  • Legal or regulatory requirements
  • Financial decisions or calculations
  • People's titles, contact info, or credentials
  • Anything where being wrong has consequences
  • Highly specialized or niche domain claims
  • Internal company policies or procedures
